Our Story

Many students are capable of more serious thought than they are usually invited into.

The project began from a simple frustration: too much education is organized around pace, performance, and simplification, and not enough around depth, difficulty, and real attention.

Why it exists

Many students do not need more simplification. They need room to think properly.

There are students who want to linger on a question, follow an argument carefully, or stay with a difficult idea until it becomes clear. In many ordinary educational settings, there is very little room for that.

Tangent tries to create those conditions: harder material, smaller tracks, better adult attention, and enough time for genuine discussion.

Tangent is not built around prestige signals. It is built around intellectual appetite.

What it believes

Students can do more

Many students can go further than institutions expect when they are given difficult material and taken seriously.

Hard material matters

Difficult books and strong ideas build taste, judgment, and habits of mind that easier substitutes often do not.

Adult attention matters

Capable adults can help students sort out confusion without flattening the material or taking over the thinking.

Independence is the goal

The aim is not endless explanation. It is gradual autonomy: reading better, thinking better, and standing more firmly on one's own understanding.

How it began

Built from real reading groups, then refined.

Tangent grew out of informal reading sessions in India, where students pushed beyond the usual classroom pace and into harder material. Some later went on to IIT programs, and others to postgraduate study and research in the USA. The format later extended to undergraduate reading groups in Germany.

The tracks are small by design, so students can be known well and the discussions can stay alive.

Influences

Some of the thinking behind Tangent draws on Paul Graham on hard problems and staying upwind, Erik Hoel on expert adult attention, and Henrik Karlsson on children being taken seriously by capable adults.

Ideas behind this: Paul Graham, Erik Hoel, and Henrik Karlsson.

Where it may grow

The larger idea is wider than the Foundational Track alone.

Foundational track

Small reading groups for students in Grades 7–12 who want depth, discussion, and stronger habits of thought.

Advanced track

Tangent may also grow into a track for older students reading papers and more technical material directly.

Frontier track

There is also room in the larger vision for a looser track built around live questions, shared reading, and intellectual community.
